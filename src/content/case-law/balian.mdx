February 1, 1988

D.B. Bayne and M. Giroux, for the Applicant.

B. Russell, for the Respondents.

**SMITH J.:—** This is an application, with
certiorari in aid. Its purpose is to attack a transfer decision made by the
respondents Warden Epp and the Regional Transfer Board on November 19, 1987,
pursuant to which the applicant was taken from Joyceville, a medium security
institution near Kingston, Ontario, to the maximum security Millhaven institution
nearby.

Counsel from the Department of Justice representing the respondents submitted
by way of a preliminary objection that habeas corpus did not lie and neither
did certiorari since the whole matter related to an administrative decision
within the confines of federal institutions and hence one for the Federal Court
to inquire into and review. I heard argument on the preliminary point when I
first became seized of the application and I reserved my decision without adverting
to the merits. I refer to merits not of the Board's decision itself, but on
the question of whether the Board had jurisdiction for I am not sitting in appeal.
My interest on habeas corpus is in the lawfulness of the accused's imprisonment
or loss of liberty. In this instance, the ground upon which the applicant relies
to urge unlawful detention is a breach of s. 7 of the Charter or a breach of
the rules of natural justice. Hence it is contended that the prison authorities
have acted without jurisdiction or have exceeded it.

After considering the point raised, I notified counsel that the Court should
convene anew for the purpose of hearing full argument as on a return of the
Writ. I had decided that I should have an evidentiary framework within which
to consider the jurisdictional issue. I could not decide it in a vacuum. The
Miller (infra) line of cases made it imperative that I look at the nature of
confinement at Millhaven as opposed to Joyceville if I was to properly assess
the extent to which the residual liberties were affected, if at all. While that
alone was not determinative, I felt it could turn out to be of significance
on the issue of whether habeas corpus was available. I was not prepared to decide
without being given more facts whether holding a prisoner in one institution
as opposed to another could ever be cause for exercizing habeas corpus jurisdiction.

Having heard the evidence, I am satisfied that the application should succeed.
I now proceed to give reasons.

Two issues present themselves immediately for consideration. They are as follows:

1. Whether a provincial superior court has jurisdiction to
issue certiorari in aid of habeas corpus to review the validity of a detention
imposed by a federal board despite the exclusive jurisdiction in respect of
certiorari of the Federal Court of Canada under s. 18 of the Federal Court Act;

2. Does habeas corpus lie to determine the validity of confinement in a maximum
security institution following an allegedly unlawful transfer from a medium
security one.

The first issue has now been settled. The Supreme Court of Canada addressed
the issue of the availability of habeas corpus in a penitentiary situation in
three cases: Miller v. The Queen (1985), 23 C.C.C. (3d) 97; Cardinal and Oswald
v. Director of Kent Institution (1985), 23 C.C.C. (3d) 119; and Morin v. National
Special Handling Unit Review Committee (1985), 23 C.C.C. (3d) 133. All three
cases were factually similar, involving an inmate's transfer from the general
prison environment to a segregated, isolated area such as a "special handling
unit". The inmates challenged their segregation on the basis of a writ
of habeas corpus with or without certiorari in aid. All three judgments were
delivered by Le Dain J.

In answer to the first question, Le Dain J. relied primarily on Laskin C.J.C.'s
dissent in Mitchell v. The Queen (1975), 24 C.C.C. (2d) 241, where he held that
s. 18 of the Federal Court Act had not removed the provincial court's jurisdiction
to issue certiorari in aid of habeas corpus against a federal board. Laskin
C.J.C. had drawn a distinction between certiorari used to quash a conviction
or an order by its own strength, and certiorari in aid of habeas corpus to make
that remedy more effective by requiring the production of the record of proceedings
for that purpose. Le Dain J. adopted this distinction explaining that s. 18
of the Act refers only to the independent remedy of certiorari to quash, a remedy
which is within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Federal Court. By contrast,
"certiorari as an ancillary procedure used to serve an essentially evidentiary
purpose" is still available to provincial superior courts. As Justice Le
Dain emphasized, the habeas corpus remedy would be ineffectual if the provincial
courts were denied the use of certiorari in aid. It is clearly an important
point of policy which transcends the jurisdictional problems the interpretation
may entail.

The court thereby resolved the jurisdictional issue in favour of the provincial
superior courts, concluding that a provincial superior court has jurisdiction
to issue certiorari in aid of habeas corpus so as to review the validity of
a detention imposed by a federal board, commission or tribunal. Naturally, Le
Dain J. reached the same conclusion in Cardinal and Morin.

I confess that at first blush it was not apparent to me why the applicant
was using the habeas corpus vehicle when a specialized court, namely the Federal
Court of Canada, lay close at hand with its rich reservoir of jurisprudence
in custodial penitentiary cases drawing on both certiorari and mandamus. Counsel
could not point to a single benefit, however trifling, that could be derived
from the alternative of resorting to a provincial court possessed of habeas
corpus jurisdiction. I make the comment in passing, fully appreciating that
nothing can turn on it. But as a matter of practicality, the Federal Court must
surely be the more appropriate forum in the vast majority of cases.

Before dealing with the second issue of the availability of habeas corpus,
the facts should be briefly stated. The applicant was sentenced to six years
in the penitentiary on June 13, 1986 for conspiring to commit the murder of
Kani Gungor, a Turkish Diplomat. He was sent to Millhaven which is classified
as a maximum security institution and remained there in the general prison population
for ten months until April 15, 1987. He was then transferred to Joyceville,
a medium security institution, by way of a voluntary transfer and remained there
until October 29, 1987.

At Millhaven, there is a "Special Handling Unit" (S.H.U.) categorized
as S7 or very high security, with privileges kept to a minimum. Millhaven itself
is S6 - Collins Bay S5 and Joyceville S4. The applicant had refused Collins
Bay for reasons that are largely irrelevant. The applicant testified to the
cascading process by which an inmate desiring to take advantage of it with the
approval of a classification officer who feels that the inmate will benefit,
works his way down the security levels through the farm annex (S3 and 2) and
onto the street.

On October 29, the applicant was made the subject of an involuntary transfer
back to Millhaven. There was no suggestion of behavioural problems of any kind
on the part of the applicant until the very events that immediately triggered
the transfer. In fact he had been a fairly model prisoner. He said that he was
disciplined and bent upon improving his intellectual and other skills. His evidence
was not challenged. He had been interviewed for S3, a transfer which he had
been prepared to accept until he toured the facilities. He had then declined
for personal reasons.

The precipitating event leading to the impugned transfer was a sit-down strike
at Joyceville which was brought on by the involuntary transfer to Millhaven
of another inmate named Demaria. The applicant and two others out of a total
of possibly 500 inmates were singled out and directed to return to work. They
refused and were sent to Millhaven as a result of their refusal. The only document
which the applicant received was a transfer notice which set out the reasons
for the action as follows:

1. Your refusal to go to work when ordered during a 4-day
sit-down at Joyceville, jeopardized the possibility of the institution returning
to normal routine.

2. There is confidential security information indicating that you are playing
a supporting role in extending the work stoppage.

3. There is confidential information indicating you are involved in illegal
drug usage.

[My emphasis.]

It is conceded at the hearing before this court that there was no information
in support of the third reason. The applicant was emphatic that he is not a
drug user. He was given no further information regarding the case against him
and therefore no basis upon which to mount a challenge of the order of transfer
either at the institution before a person or body in authority or elsewhere
and either then or later. He is still without any real knowledge of the specific
charges or of the case to meet.

The notice called for rebuttal by the applicant within 72 hours. He wrote
a letter in which he vehemently denied the third allegation and stated that
his participation in the sit-down strike was no more or less than that of any
other inmate. A blanket denial was indeed his only recourse in the face of the
paucity of information available to him.

There are two subsumed questions on the second issue which the above facts
raise, namely procedural fairness, and related to it, whether, in the words
of Le Dain J. in Miller, the transfer to Millhaven involved a significant reduction
in the residual liberty of the inmate. The Minister argues on behalf of the
respondents that there is only involved here at its worse a loss of privilege
which according to Miller should not be accepted as sufficient to permit a challenge
on habeas corpus.

I will deal with the second question first. I think it is noteworthy that
the three cases, Miller, Cardinal and Morin, supra, were factually similar,
involving an inmate's transfer from the general prison environment to a segregated
isolated area. The concept of "a prison within a prison" approach
was utilized. The Court drew on the book by R.J. Sharpe, The Law of Habeas Corpus
(1976) Oxford Clarendon Press, at pp. 149-150:

While the courts have been reluctant to act in the past,
it is submitted that the fact that a prisoner cannot be released altogether
should be no reason to refuse relief on habeas corpus. If, for example, a prisoner
is improperly put in solitary confinement ... there seems to be no reason why
he should not be able to use habeas corpus to be freed from that restraint.
The situation may be seen as a 'prison within a prison' and the applicant is
simply released from the inner prison while being kept within the confines of
the outer one....

The important point is that if there is need for a judicial remedy, habeas corpus
could be used effectively. The writ is nothing more than a command to the goaler
from the court to have the prisoner brought before it to justify the detention.
It is but a small step to extend this to cases where a measure of restraint
is unquestionably justified but where the prisoner contends that the conditions
of imprisonment are improper, and requires the goaler to justify the imposition
of those conditions.

It bears repetition that Le Dain J. cautioned against the use of habeas corpus
"to challenge any and all conditions of confinement in a penitentiary or
prison". Indeed habeas corpus is conceived as a method of obtaining a prisoner's
release either from confinement simpliciter or on the basis of Miller and other
cases, from a new and different detention that rests on its own foundation within
a larger context of confinement. It becomes more difficult therefore, but not
inconceivable, that the remedy should be extended to a mere loss of a privilege
or privileges. Each case will be decided on its facts.

In the instant case the concept of "releasing" creates no difficulty.
It is simply, in my view, a logical extension of Miller to place within the
reach of habeas corpus a release from one institution to another within the
same system as long as the deprivation of an inmate's residual liberty is significant.

It has been said in a commentary on the Miller case by Professor Manson, (1986),
16 Admin. L.R. 285 at p. 289, that Le Dain J.'s judgments in the trilogy stand
for the following broad proposition:

... Le Dain J. hinges the availability of habeas corpus
to any distinct form of detention which "involves" a significant reduction
in the residual liberty of the inmate. Thus, habeas corpus is entrenched as
the remedy to protect against deprivations of liberty not only in an absolute
sense but in all situations where significantly more onerous constraints are
placed on individuals.

Interestingly, the same sentiment is expressed in Re Bell and Director of
Springhill Medium Security Institution (1977), 34 C.C.C. (2d) 303 (N.S.C.A.),
a case Mr. Justice Le Dain referred to in Miller. It is a Nova Scotia Court
of Appeal decision in which habeas corpus was used to successfully challenge
a prisoner's transfer from one penitentiary to another. The applicant argued
that the transfer was not statutorily authorized since the penal institution
to which he was transferred was not a "penitentiary" as defined by
the Penitentiary Act. What is interesting about the case is Chief Justice MacKeigan's
conception of the uses to which habeas corpus can be put (at p. 305):

As stated by Mr. Cameron Harvey in his excellent study,
The Law of Habeas Corpus in Canada (1974), p. 41:

"... habeas corpus is potentially available to test
the legality of every conceivable actual detention". On habeas corpus a
Court is required to determine if a prisoner is detained on the order of a competent
authority and according to law, and to order him discharged from any custody
found to be unlawful."

Le Dain J. seemingly quotes that case with approval.

I am satisfied that the deprivation in this case is not trivial. The word
"significant" as used in Miller is not a word of art. It must mean
what it says, i.e., significant or substantial. The test would appear to be
met without the necessity of evidence when the transfer is from a medium security
institution to a maximum one. But in this case I have heard considerable evidence
as to the differences between the two institutions. I need only advert to a
few. The nature of the institution to which an inmate is committed when he applies
for parole will play a role in the outcome of his application. Searching of
inmates is much more frequent at Millhaven than at Joyceville and in certain
instances strip searching is engaged in. The make-up of the population, of necessity,
is quite different and as a result the overall admosphere is palpably less oriented
to rehabilitation and eventual return to society. The inmates have longer terms
to serve and will tend to be more desperate. I infer from the evidence that
most of the rules governing daily life at Joyceville, from the nature of the
clothing permitted, to library, canteen, gym and eating activities, are somewhat
more relaxed. The conditions surrounding in person visiting and telephoning
are less stringent. I hold with little hesitation that by being transferred
to Millhaven, the applicant suffered a significant reduction in his residual
liberties.

That leaves only the question of procedural fairness unresolved. In the context
of an involuntary transfer decision, the rules of natural justice require as
a minimum the submission of sufficient information for the person affected to
enable him to effectively defend himself against possible arbitrariness. He
must be notified of the case to meet. It is only in this way that the recognition
of his right to make representations will be meaningful. Emergency situations
must be recognized as permitting the authorities to derogate temporarily from
the rules. But at some point and at the earliest possible moment, any arbitrariness
or suspicion of arbitrariness must be promptly addressed if they are found to
exist by applying the rules. They are now well known having been so often articulated
in the cases as I have just done.

The penitentiary authorities need not feel the erosion of their authority.
In them vests the difficult, complex and very demanding task of running a penal
institution and the courts should be loathe to interfere. In fact they will
not, so long as punitive measures - it was virtually conceded that the transfer
was punitive here - are taken only after the alleged misconduct or the charges
to support them are proven according to what has come to be compendiously called
procedural fairness or natural justice. The use of informer evidence does not
act as justification for dispensing with fairness. Experience has shown that
a great deal can be disclosed while protecting the identity of the fellow inmates
who inform.

This case in my view justifies judicial intervention. The facts summarized
earlier show that the right of the applicant to defend himself was ignored.
The transfer will be quashed and the applicant is ordered returned to Joyceville.

There is nothing to prevent the authorities from undertaking new proceedings
if they are so advised upon proper notification to the applicant and the observance
of the minimum requirements which natural justice dictates. I do not think this
is a case for costs.

**STEELE J.**

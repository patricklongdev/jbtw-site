Judgment: June 14, 1995.

Appeal from judgment of Desjardins Prov. Ct. J., New Brunswick Provincial Court,
April 3, 1995.

*Civil rights — Detention and imprisonment — Detention
— What constitutes arbitrary detention — Bail and interim release
— Right to be taken before a judge within 24 hours — Denial of right
— Remedies — Reduction of sentence.*

This was an appeal from the conviction of the accused on charges of possession
of property obtained by crime and driving while disqualified. The accused was
arrested on a Friday and was taken in front of a Provincial Court Judge on the
following Monday. He pleaded guilty at that time but also argued that his rights
under section 503 of the Criminal Code and section 9 of the Canadian Charter
of Rights and Freedoms had been violated in that he was not taken before a Provincial
Court Judge as soon as possible after his arrest as required by section 503
of the Code.

**HELD:** The appeal from conviction was dismissed.
The application under section 24(1) of the Charter was allowed. The sentence
imposed on the accused for the possession of property conviction was reduced
from six months to three months. Even though it was unlikely that the accused
would have been released on bail if he had been taken before a judge before
the Monday, his right not to be arbitrarily detained under section 9 of the
Charter was breached by the failure to bring him before a judge within 24 hours.
The delay was caused by the fact that there were no judges available on the
weekends in New Brunswick. This systemic defect required that the accused be
granted a remedy. Given the considerable criminal record of the accused, it
was not appropriate to stay the charges. The appropriate remedy under section
24(1) was to substantially reduce his sentence.

Statutes, Regulations and Rules Cited:

Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 1982, ss. 9, 24(1).
Criminal Code, ss. 259, 354(1), 503, 503(a).

**RYAN J.A.:—**Norman Hector MacPherson was
stopped in Florenceville, Judicial District of Woodstock, New Brunswick, driving
a car stolen in Nova Scotia. He was en route to Ontario with his girlfriend.
He was born September 8, 1963 and has as many criminal convictions as he has
years.
MacPherson was arrested on Friday, March 31, 1995, kept in jail over the week-end,
and was taken before a Provincial Court judge on Monday, April 3 at Perth-Andover.
He pleaded guilty to possession of property obtained by crime, s.354(1), and
to driving while disqualified, s.259 of the Criminal Code of Canada. To no avail,
he complained to the Provincial Court judge that his rights under s.9 of the
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms and under s.503 of the Criminal Code
of Canada had been violated. The judge sentenced him to serve a term of imprisonment
of six months on the possession charge and two months on the driving charge,
the latter to be served concurrently. MacPherson has lodged a prisoner appeal
of his convictions claiming that the Provincial Court had no jurisdiction to
sentence him because his rights were violated.
The main exchange between the court of first instance and the accused on this
point was as follows:
THE COURT: Your big concern a while ago was for Miss Enright.

MR. MACPHERSON: That and a habeas corpus issue of a writ, your honour, that
I thought may avail me something because if, I don't know if it should be mentioned
here or not, your honour. In other Provinces, per se, they have you in front
of a J.P. within twenty-four hours. They guarantee it. They make sure that you
do that. I was arrested on a Friday as such. It doesn't happen in this here...

THE COURT: That's right.
MR. MACPHERSON: Province, and I think it's...it's not so
much law as it is just unwritten policy that happens around here and well, the
judge is not here and we don't have J.P. so it doesn't happen here but as far
as the law goes, it would have been possible to have been brought in front of
a Justice of the Peace.

THE COURT: In Nova Scotia or Ontario, or in provinces in which they have J.P.'s
but...

MR. MACPHERSON: Yeah.
THE COURT: Not in New Brunswick because it says the first...within
twenty-four hours or within the first, and Saturdays and Sundays don't count
as...in those twenty-four hours because there...

MR. MACPHERSON: Or as soon as possible, it would be possible...

THE COURT: Which is Monday morning.
MR. MACPHERSON: Yeah, that's conveniency.
THE COURT: Well...
MR. MACPHERSON: That's not possibility. That's...I think
there's distinction between the two, you know. It's been going on like this
for years and it hasn't changed as such in this province as it has in other
provinces but I think there is ... there is a ... there is a case there for
a habeas corpus if I wish to push it and that was one of my main concern. That's
all as far as that goes, your honour.

For whatever reason, the judge failed to deal directly with MacPherson's complaint.

Section 9 of the Charter prohibits arbitrary detention and s.503 of the Criminal
Code requires that all arrested persons, if not released by the police, be taken
before a Provincial Court judge within 24 hours of arrest or as soon as possible
if a judge is not available.
9. Everyone has the right not to be arbitrarily detained or
imprisoned.

503. (1) A peace officer who arrests a person with or without warrant or to
whom a person is delivered under subsection 494(3) shall cause the person to
be detained in custody and, in accordance with the following provisions, to
be taken before a justice to be dealt with according to law, namely,

(a) where a justice is available within a period of twenty-four
hours after the person has been arrested by or delivered to the peace officer,
the person shall be taken before a justice without unreasonable delay and in
any event within that period, and

(b) where a justice is not available within a period of twenty-four hours after
the person has been arrested by or delivered to the peace officer, the person
shall be taken before a justice as soon as possible.

unless, at any time before the expiration of the time prescribed
in paragraph (a) or (b) for taking the person before a justice,

(c) the peace officer or officer in charge releases the person
under any other provision of this Part, or

(d) the peace officer or officer in charge is satisfied that the person should
be released from custody, whether unconditionally under sub-section (4) or otherwise
conditionally or unconditionally, and so releases him.

Section 503(a) requires that, in New Brunswick, every person under arrest
be taken before a provincial court judge without unreasonable delay, and in
any event within 24 hours. The 24 hour time frame in sub-clause (a) is being
construed strictly by the courts. One of the purposes of a strict interpretation
is so that persons will not languish in jail on weekends. For example, we were
informed that at Woodstock where MacPherson would ordinarily have appeared in
court, a provincial court judge is unavailable between Friday of one week and
Tuesday of the following week. For this reason, MacPherson was taken to Perth-Andover
on the Monday following his arrest. This situation is not unique to Woodstock
but applies in other areas of the Province.
There are no justices of the peace in New Brunswick and only 26 full time
judges of a busy Provincial Court. The province has no provision for justices
of the peace, an archaic system which was discontinued in the 1960's, but undoubtedly
serves some useful purpose in other provinces with different population and
geographic centers than New Brunswick. The system of deputy provincial court
judges was also discontinued for reasons of substance. In addition, the Province
recently discontinued the supernumerary status for judges who have attained
65 years of age.
In any event, there are areas in the Province which are not serviced on weekends
by the Provincial Courts, a systemic defect.
A person under arrest is supposed to be taken before a judge without unreasonable
delay, and in any event within 24 hours. When an accused is not, his or her
continued detention becomes arbitrary and, therefore, a violation of s.9 of
the Charter which prohibits arbitrary detention.
The Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Simpson, [1995] 1 S.C.R. 449 in a terse
judgment upheld the trial judge and reversed a majority decision of the Newfoundland
Court of Appeal. In that case the 21 year old accused had been arrested on charges
of assault causing bodily harm and of public mischief in making a false allegation
of assault. The accused was arrested on Sunday afternoon. Monday was a holiday
and so the accused was not taken before a judge until Tuesday. There was no
system for judges to be available on weekends for the purposes of releasing
detainees on condition. The police objected to the release of the accused unless
she was released on condition that she stay away from certain persons. On Tuesday
she was released upon this condition.
Subsequently, the trial judge held that her right, under s.9 of the Charter,
not to be arbitrarily detained, had been violated. He entered a stay of the
proceedings. In a brief decision, the majority of the Newfoundland Court of
Appeal set aside the stay. The majority held that her remedy under s.24(1) of
the Charter was to sue civilly. Mr. Justice O'Neill, in dissent, held otherwise.
He said in (1994) 29 C.R. (4th) 274 at 275, and I cite the entire judgment in
dissent:
There had been a clear and serious breach of s.9 and it
could not be said that the trial judge was in error in exercising his discretion
under s.24(1) of the Charter to stay the proceedings. He had thoroughly canvassed
various other remedies, such as reduction of sentence and damages, and concluded
that no remedy short of a stay would be appropriate and just. Appropriate civil
damages would require an application in the Supreme Court with increased legal
fees, delay and inconvenience. An arbitrary deprivation of liberty is not acceptable
and affording no remedy would condone the deprivation of liberty.

In an annotation to the Simpson case, Prof. Don Stuart, Editor-in-Chief of
the Criminal Reports said at page 276:
Under the Charter, it is trite that issues do arise in
the criminal trial independent of guilt. The important remedy of exclusion of
evidence under s.24(2)has often resulted in the acquittal of the guilty. Furthermore,
the Supreme Court in R. v. Strachan, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 980, 67 C.R. (3d) 878,
[1989] 1 W.W.R. 385, 90 N.R. 273, 46 C.C.C. (3d) 479, 56 D.L.R. (4th) 673, 37
C.R.R. 335, has in that context rejected a strict causal test, even though s.24(2)
speaks of a possibility of exclusion of evidence "obtained in a manner
that infringed or denied: any Charter right. In the case of a breach of such
important protections as that against arbitrary detention, the courts should
be receptive to a meaningful remedy under the open-ended s. 24(1) such as a
stay or, in some cases, a reduction in sentence. It is disingenuous to rely
on the availability of a civil remedy for damages given the difficulty and cost
of bringing such actions against authorities and the sparse record of success
both before and after the Charter.

Section 24(1) of the Charter is as follows:
24. (1) Anyone whose rights or freedoms, as guaranteed by
this Charter, have been infringed or denied may apply to a court of competent
jurisdiction to obtain such remedy as the court considers appropriate and just
in the circumstances.

The Supreme Court of Canada heard the appeal as of right, and in the vast
majority of appeals now heard by it "as of right", handed down its
decision orally. In a two sentence judgment the five judge panel held:
**LAMER C.J. -**With respect,
we are all of the view that there was no reason for interfering with the exercise
of discretion by the trial judge. The appeal is therefore allowed and the stay
entered by the trial judge is restored.

In New Brunswick, as was the case in Newfoundland, there is no system in place
for judges to be available on weekends. This is a systemic defect which, until
now, has resulted in arrested persons wrongly being detained in violation of
s.9 of the Charter when they are not taken before a judge as required under
s. 503 of the Criminal Code. Since it is a systemic defect in the judicial district,
it does not, as perhaps the judge of first instance thought, trigger a discussion
of s.503(1)(b) "where a justice is not available within a period of twenty-four
hours after the person has been arrested. . .". Justice does not stop on
weekends.
In R. v. Charles (1987), 59 C.R. (3d) 94 the Saskatchewan Court of Appeal
upheld the substantial reduction of a sentence which normally would have been
imposed on an assault charge. They declined to quash the conviction. However,
under the recent Simpson decision, a stay also would have been considered appropriate.

Here, MacPherson has a lengthy record going from coast to coast. He told us
that there is an outstanding warrant for his arrest for failing to appear in
a court in British Columbia. At the time of his arrest in Florenceville he was
passing through New Brunswick in a stolen car. His chances of being released,
even if a judge had been available, were, to put it in perspective, negligible.
Be that as it may, his rights to be taken before a judge within 24 hours and
not to be arbitrarily detained, were violated. Under the circumstances, and
to emphasize that a violation of this Charter right is serious, I would dismiss
his appeal against convictions but allow his application under s.24(1) of the
Charter of Rights and Freedoms. I would substantially reduce his sentence by
setting aside the sentence of six months under s. 354(1) and impose a sentence
of three months. I would not disturb the sentence of two months imposed under
s.259 of the Criminal Code.
As noted by Prof. Stuart in his annotation, Lamer J. (as he then was) in R.
v. Mills, [1986] 1 S.C.R 863 recognized that the power of a court of competent
jurisdiction to give "appropriate and just" remedies under s. 24(1)
for Charter violations establishes the:
. . . right to a remedy as the foundation stone for the
effective enforcement of Charter rights.

See also the decision of this court in R. v. Dennison (1991), 109 N.B.R.(2d)
388, application for leave to appeal refused by the Supreme Court of Canada
(1991), 111 N.B.R.(2d)90, where the sentence was reduced under s.24(1) because
of the refusal of the sentencing judge to permit the accused to speak to sentence.

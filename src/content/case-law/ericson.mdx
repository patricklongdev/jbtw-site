**SPENCER J.:—** This is an application for
habeas corpus with certiorari in aid to quash the decision of the Warden of
the Elbow Lake Correctional Institute to transfer the petitioner to a more secure
penal institution. According to the evidence before me the petitioner is serving
a five year sentence for two counts of the possession of stolen property. His
sentence began in November 1990. In February 1991 he was sent to the Elbow Lake
Institution, a level 2 minimum security establishment. He has had a good record
of work and behaviour while incarcerated. In particular, he has been able to
receive many visits from his family and friends there, has enjoyed a number
of social and work related passes and has become involved in writing a script
for and advising a film company with respect to a film series about crime. He
has the opportunity to pursue his writing with that company after his eventual
release. His transfer to the Matsqui Institution, a level 5 institution, has
interrupted that work opportunity, has generally subjected him to a greater
degree of security while incarcerated and has reduced the privileges available
to him.
At the heart of the petitioner's complaint is the allegation that he was transferred
without being given an opportunity to be heard in opposition to the charges
levelled against him that caused his transfer. All he was told was that he was
named by several inmates at Elbow Lake as the person responsible for the traffic
of narcotics within that institution and that therefore he, together with others,
was being transferred to other prisons. Without knowing any details of that
allegation he strenuously denied it. His denial was rejected and his transfer
was confirmed.
Any review of a decision by the custodial authorities with respect to the
place where a prisoner should be held and the prison discipline that should
be imposed upon him must proceed cautiously, in the perspective that those who
run a prison are in a peculiarly advantageous position to know what is happening
there and to assess its significance. In particular, where, as here, the authority
says that to give further details of the allegations against an inmate would
reveal the source of confidential communications and expose another inmate to
the risk of retaliation, that opinion should not lightly be rejected. At the
same time, it must be remembered that the courts have jurisdiction over these
questions in a broad sense and a mandate to ensure that the prison authority
observes the rules of procedural fairness, see R. vs. Miller [1985] 49 C.R.
(3d) 1 (S.C.C.) and Cardinal and Oswald vs. Director of Kent Institution [1985]
49 C.R. (3d) 35 (S.C.C.). Counsel for the respondents concedes as much and opposed
this petition solely on its facts, saying that as much as could be done without
jeopardizing the safety of informants has been done.
I find nothing flawed in the way in which the respondents reacted to what
appeared to be an emergency situation in the Elbow Lake Institution by transferring
the petitioner out pending a further enquiry. There was evidence of an increasing
drug problem in the institution, marked by incidents of overdose by two inmates
and by the accidental death of a third. A number of inmates anonymously named
the petitioner as the organizer of trafficking into the institution and he was
therefore transferred. All he was told was this:
"Information has been received from several different
sources that you are trafficking in drugs in Elbow Lake Institution. Your case
will be reviewed for reclassification and/or return to Elbow Lake Institution
upon completion of our investigation." Upon receipt of that notice the
petitioner denied the allegation.
According to the evidence there then followed three investigations, one by
the authorities at Elbow Lake, one by an official from another penal institution
and one by the R.C.M.P. As a result, the petitioner was given another notice
which read:
"Your involvement in the drug trade at ELI has been
identified by several inmate sources. Given this information and subsequent
information that the appropriate inmates have been removed from the institution
lead (sic) to the conclusion that you present too great a risk to remain in
a minimum security environment. You were initially transferred to Kent Institution
pending the outcome of an investigation and until bed space could be arranged."

The effect of that notice was to confirm his transfer away from Elbow Lake
and the consequent loss of some of the privileges he enjoyed there.
There is also evidence that in an interview with one of the Elbow Lake staff,
the petitioner was told words to the effect that he had been named by other
inmates as the "main man" of the drug trafficking there.
It is immediately apparent that the petitioner, while denying the allegations
in a general sense, had no idea of the identity of those who named his as a
trafficker or of the nature of their evidence against him. I accept that to
name the informants, other inmates of the penal system, might well expose them
to the risk of retaliation and injury. The prison staff owes a duty to protect
them. I accept also that the staff, who know the precise nature of the allegations
made against the petitioner, are in the best position to judge what detail may
and may not be given to the petitioner without jeopardizing the safety of those
other inmates. But the court has a role to play in ensuring that the petitioner
has been fairly treated, and fair treatment requires as much as possible that
he know some detail of the charges against him so that he can respond to them.
Otherwise he finds himself caught in the strange world described, in its extreme,
by Franz Kafka in "The Trial" (1925) see Library of Congress No. 68-59195.

Here, the petitioner knew he was alleged to be a leader of the prison drug
trade. He has no record of conviction in any drug related matter nor of any
prior disciplinary proceeding in the prison system. His denial of any involvement
in the allegations against him is responded to in these words by the Preventative
Security Co-Ordinator who interviewed him during the review of the decision
to transfer him:
"I offered the petitioner the opportunity to verbally
respond to the allegations. He denied the allegations but did not provide further
explanation, indicating that the allegations were "preposterous" and
stating "you'll hear from my lawyer"."

Unless the petitioner were given some detail of what had been said against
him it is difficult to imagine what further explanation he might have given.

The prison authorities are undoubtedly faced with a very delicate task in
deciding what detail can safely be given to a prisoner such as the petitioner
without jeopardizing the safety of others. I am not privy to what the respondents
know to make that judgment myself. Nor am I given the role of deciding whether
the decision reached by the respondents on the merits of the case was right
or wrong. My task is simply to ensure that the petitioner was fairly treated.

With all due deference to the respondents, I cannot think that the petitioner
could not have been given greater detail here. If other inmates have informed
against him, it is reasonable to suppose they did not implicate themselves in
any wrongdoing. So, probably, their evidence was hearsay. If they informed about
the petitioner's reputation within the institution they could have nothing to
say that would identify them. If the reputation they spoke of identified the
petitioner with any specific activity he should have been given that detail
so that he would know from what it was he must defend himself. I think it unlikely
that a drug trafficking operation within a prison is so devoid of system that
specific allegations cannot be put to a suspect. I incline to the view expressed
by Smith J. in Balian v. Regional Transfer Board et al. (1988), 62 C.R. (3d)
258 at 266, "that experience has shown that a great deal can be disclosed
while protecting the identity of fellow inmates who inform". I am not convinced
that in this case the convergence of evidence from eight inmates, otherwise
said to be unrelated to each other, and the coinciding findings of the three
separate investigations points so inexorably to the petitioner's guilt that
he must have been guilty. All eight inmates may have listened to the same source.
All three investigators may have interviewed the same informants. There is no
way of telling. What is clear is that the petitioner was not permitted to play
a reasonably informed part in the whole process. That was procedurally unfair
to him.
Counsel for the respondents sought to rely on the decision of the Federal
Court of Appeal in Gallant v. Correctional Service Canada (Pacific Region) (1989),
92 N.R. 292. The court there, at page 301, drew a distinction between the case
of a single infraction leading to a particular punishment, the details of which
should be given to an inmate to permit him a chance to answer it, and a continuing
series of wrongdoings that leads to a transfer of the inmate for the sake of
the orderly and proper administration of the institution. I must confess to
some difficulty in appreciating the difference, but in any event what was done
here was done as a result of a single endeavour alleged against the petitioner,
not the result of "all the wrongdoings" referred to in that case.
Certainly it was done with a view to protecting the integrity of the Elbow Lake
Institution as a whole, but the petitioner was singled out for that purpose
without being given a real opportunity to defend himself.
I wish to emphasize that I do not denigrate the role of the prison authorities
in holding the delicate balance between the right of the individual inmate to
fair treatment and the rights of informants to be kept secret and secure. Nor
do I diminish in any sense the very heavy responsibility laid upon them to administer
a difficult community. I simply say that the almost total absence of detail
supplied to the petitioner of the allegations against him, which lead to a very
significant change in his custodial status and privileges and in his opportunities
for rehabilitation persuade me that his petition should be granted. There will
be an order accordingly to quash the decision of the Warden of the Elbow Lake
Institution and an order that he be returned either there, or to some other
appropriate institution with the same security classification.

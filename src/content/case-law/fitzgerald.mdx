*Prisons -- Administration -- Powers re prisoners -- Transfers
-- Grounds -- Whether decision to transfer was patently unreasonable.*

Petition for an order transferring a prison inmate from one institution to
a higher security institution. The petitioner was serving a life sentence with
no eligibility for parole for 25 years. Based on information received from another
inmate that the petitioner was planning an escape, the defendants decided to
transfer him to a maximum security prison. The petitioner was unable to show
that the informant lied, because the respondent refused to disclose the identity
of the informant. The petitioner's case management team had earlier recommended
his transfer to a minimum security prison on the ground that substantial progress
had been made in the rehabilitation of the petitioner. The alleged escape plot
was so inept as to seem unbelievable. The issue for determination was whether
the decision of the respondent met the tests of procedural fairness.

**HELD**: Petition allowed. The court was unable
to conclude that the respondent's reasons properly reflected the evidence, including
such information as was concealed from the petitioner. The decision to transfer
appeared to have been extremely arbitrary and, as such, unfair. It had not been
demonstrated that the decision was arrived at fairly and, accordingly, the decision
was patently unreasonable.

Statutes, Regulations and Rules Cited:

Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 1982, s. 7. Corrections and Conditional
Release Act, s. 70.

Counsel for the Petitioner: John W. Conroy.

Counsel for the Respondents: David Fitzsimmons.

**THACKRAY J.:—** The petitioner seeks an order
transferring him from Kent Institution to William Head Institution. Mr. Fitzgerald
was convicted in 1981 of first degree murder which occurred during a robbery.
A gas station attendant and a police officer were shot and killed by Mr. Fitzgerald.
He was sentenced to life imprisonment with no eligibility for parole for 25
years. In 1984 he was found guilty by an Internal Disciplinary Court of attempted
escape from Kingston Penitentiary.

PETITION

The petitioner applies to this court for an order for relief
in the nature of habeas corpus pursuant to sections 774 and 482(3)(c) of the
Criminal Code of Canada, the Criminal Rules, 1991 and Rules 10 and 63 of the
Rules of Court and sections 10(c) and 24(1) of the Canadian Charter of Rights
and Freedoms, directed to the Warden of Kent Institution, ordering that the
petitioner be released from Kent Institution and transferred back to William
Head Institution relying on the relevant sections of the Penitentiary Act, the
Penitentiary Service Regulations, the Corrections and Conditional Release Act
and the Corrections and Conditional Release Act Regulations as may apply.

HISTORY OF PETITIONS

The first petition was filed November 30, 1992. It alleged breaches of the
Criminal Code, Penitentiary Act and Charter. However, its thrust was that insufficient
information had been provided to the petitioner. That petition was heard by
Gow J. on January 6, 1993. Reasons were delivered on January 15, 1993, Victoria
Registry No. 92 4007.

Those reasons reproduce portions of the Directive of the Commissioner of the
Correctional Service of Canada with respect to the transfer of inmates. As well,
they reproduce the Notification of Recommendation for Involuntary Transfer to
a High Maximum Security Facility, the reasons of the Warden, the confirming
reasons of the Assistant Deputy Commissioner, portions of affidavits from the
petitioner and the Warden and a list of the authorities referred to by Gow J.

Mr. Justice Gow concluded as follows:

It clearly appears from the Warden's affidavit that what
compelled him to take the steps he did was the "plan to escape" as told him
by the individual who had heard it first hand. The issue before this court is
- did the Warden's notification to the petitioner provide to the petitioner
to the greatest extent possible the substance and details of that plan as told
by that individual to the Warden?

For the court to decide that issue it must be fully informed
as to what that individual told the Warden. The laconic paragraph 5 of the Warden's
affidavit does not provide that full information. Nor can that full information
be gathered from the language of the notification itself. That language can
be construed as meaning no more than that the individual told the Warden "Fitzgerald
has told me that he plans to obtain a set of false identification and then to
escape by boat and travel to Spain" or that he told him, as the notification
suggests, much more by way of details of and about the plan.

If this matter is to be dealt with properly, then the Warden
must file by 12:00 Noon, Monday, January 25, 1993 a supplementary affidavit
containing the full information required, namely, where, when and how, who told
him what. If the individual's identity requires to be protected, that should
be stated and why. "The mere fact that the information was provided in confidence
is not in itself, sufficient reason to justify non-disclosure of that information",
re Cadieux and Director of Mountain Institution (1984), 13 C.C.C. (3d), 330
at p. 346. If immunity from disclosure is sought, then I commend to counsel
for the Warden the procedure of the "sealed envelope together with the specific
explanation" referred to in Cadieux, supra, at p. 349.

If no affidavit is filed within the time stated, then I
shall assume that the Warden does not desire to provide further information.
In that event, I shall allow the application, quash the transfer decision and
order that the petitioner be transferred back to the William Head Institution.
If the affidavit is filed, I shall consider it and, if need be, or counsel so
desire, hear further submissions before I decide the issue.

On January 20, 1993 Mr. A.M. Trono, Warden of William Head Institution, swore
an affidavit. This was followed by affidavits in support of the petitioner.
Mr. Justice Gow was provided with the internal investigation report into the
alleged escape plan. He sealed that report. On February 5 Gow J. held a further
hearing and issued reasons on February 25, 1993 in which he said:

The primary issue is whether the Warden's notification handed
to the petitioner on October 19, provided to him sufficient information about
the allegation that he had been planning to escape from William Head as to enable
him to properly reply thereto, Bruce v. Canada (1992) 13 C.R. (4th) 384 (N.B.C.A.)
or, to resort to the language of the Commissioner's directive, was there such
a "sharing" with the petitioner of the information the Warden had that the details
of the incident which prompted the transfer were provided to the greatest extent
possible.

Gow J. concluded:

In the context of the authorities referred to in my earlier
reasons and the prodigious sweep of the language of the Commissioner's directive,
I am compelled to conclude that the information provided on October 19, 1992
was insufficient. The Warden should have provided then what is now provided
in his supplementary affidavit.

Gow J. also made an important finding as to the "sharing" of information:

... now that by the supplementary affidavit there has been
a sharing to the greatest extent possible of the details of the alleged plan
to escape, the petitioner should be given the opportunity to make a response
as provided for by paragraph 15 of the Commissioner's directive, and should
he do so, the Warden must consider it.

The reasons then provide that upon receipt by the Warden of the petitioner's
response, the decisions of the Warden and of the Assistant Deputy Commissioner
"are quashed".

On March 1, 1993 the petitioner, through counsel, made such a response. On
March 10 the Acting Warden notified the petitioner that a "staff review" led
him to conclude that the transfer should be upheld. On April 16, 1993 the Assistant
Deputy Commissioner issued a Notification of Transfer Decision. He approved
the transfer to "Kent Institution, maximum security".

ISSUE OF RES JUDICATA

The respondents submitted that the whole matter is res judicata, having already
been decided by Gow J. I did not uphold the objection in that Gow J. had not
considered the "new" decisions. However, that did not mean that the decisions
of Mr. Justice Gow were subject to review. That can be done only by an appeal.

Mr. Justice Gow declined "to quash the emergency transfer decision". He also
held that "there has been a sharing to the greatest extent possible of the details
of the alleged plan to escape." Consequently, the matters of the appropriateness
of the emergency transfer and extent of the information provided are not open
for review.

The petitioner further submitted that he has been prejudiced by the respondent's
failure to charge him with a criminal offence or by the failure to proceed against
him pursuant to the disciplinary sections of the Corrections and Conditional
Release Act, S.C. 1992, c-20 and its regulations. Gow J. did not, in his reasons,
discuss this issue. However, he found nothing wanting with the procedures other
than the failure to provide adequate information in a timely manner. He carefully
set out and reviewed the Directives that were followed. Implicit in his decisions
is that the appropriate procedures, except as specifically stated, were followed
up to the juncture of his judgments.

The same applies to the petitioner's submissions that his "sense of personal
dignity" was breached contrary to s. 70 of the Corrections Act. It also applies
to the contention that there was not compliance with the Commissioner's Directive
540.

I am of the opinion that Mr. Justice Gow's findings of fact and law, whether
explicit or implicit, are binding upon me pursuant to the principles in Re Hansard
Spruce Mills Limited [1954] 13 W.W.R. (N.S.) 285.

JUDICIAL REVIEW AND SECTION 7 OF THE CHARTER

The petitioner submitted as follows:

The petitioner says that his incarceration at Kent Institution
is unlawful and that the decision to transfer him from William Head Institution
was made in violation of the common law duty to act fairly and the principles
of fundamental justice and was in violation of his constitutional rights pursuant
to section 7 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms and in violation
of the respondents' own rules of procedure and further, that in all of the circumstances,
the decision was patently unreasonable.

The "patently unreasonable test" is commonly applied in applications asking
for a judicial review. While the Judicial Review Procedure Act, R.S.B.C. 1979,
c. 209 was neither pleaded nor even mentioned on this hearing, both counsel
treated the matter as such a review.

The petitioner submitted that in judicial reviews "an unreasonable error of
fact is an error of law." He cited Blanchard v. Control Data Canada Ltd. [1984]
2 S.C.R. 476 (S.C.C.) which establishes the proposition that an unreasonable
finding, whatever its origin, affects the jurisdiction of the tribunal.

The petitioner's submission contains the following:

Reasonableness in a carceral setting and, specifically,
in relation to a decision of the National Parole Board was considered by Mr.
Justice Walsh in Re Litwack and the National Parole Board. There, the court
found that the National Parole Board's decision to refuse to vary their previous
decision in the face of strong support by those writing reports about the parolee
became so patently unfair as to require the intervention of the court. In setting
aside the decision of the Board, Mr. Justice Walsh said:

"... It is not sufficient to comply merely with procedural
fairness but also the substance of the decision must be reasonable on the facts."
(Re Litwack and the National Parole Board (1986), 26 C.C.C. (3d) 65 at pp. 71
and 76 (F.C.T.D.)).

In Demaria v. Regional Transfer Board and Warden of Joyceville
Institution, Reed, J., in a transfer case, expressed the view that both the
Federal Court of Appeal and the Supreme Court of Canada have interpreted section
7 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms as requiring not only that
there be procedural fairness, in the narrow sense, but also that decisions not
be made in an unreasonable or arbitrary manner. In her view, setting aside a
decision of an administrative body on the ground that it is arbitrary or unreasonable
as having been made without evidence to support it is one of the traditional
grounds of judicial review and as such, within the concept of "fundamental justice".
(Demaria v. Regional Transfer Board and Warden of Joyceville Institution (1898),
62 C.R. (3d) 248 at pp. 254-255 (F.C.T.D.)).

Counsel for the respondents paraphrased the words of Mr. Justice Seaton who,
in a paper suggesting a definition for "patently unreasonable", said:

I do not think it can now be said that patently unreasonable
errors are errors of a particular type as opposed to errors of a particular
magnitude. I think that the test is rather, is this a decision that we can let
stand?

Counsel for the respondents worded it this way: "Can the Court live with this
decision, i.e. is it patently unreasonable?"

While counsel agreed to the application of the patently unreasonable test,
they did not agree as to the standard to be applied. Counsel for the petitioner
submitted that it should be "beyond a reasonable doubt or something very close
thereto." He said that "just because it is administrative law, i.e. judicial
review, does not change the underlying character of the matter which is criminal."

Counsel for the respondents contended that this hearing is by way of judicial
review and that the standard to be applied is a balance of probabilities. I
agree. The respondents carry the onus of establishing that the ultimate decision
to transfer was, on a balance of probabilities, not patently unreasonable.

With respect to s. 7 of the Charter, it is well established that there is
a requirement of fairness in the making of decisions to transfer inmates to
higher security institutions. In Trono v. Gallant (1989) 68 C.R. (3d) 173 (Fed.
C.A.) at page 180 Mr. Justice Pratte said:

Since the judgment of the Supreme Court of Canada in Martineau
v. Matsqui Inst. Disciplinary Bd., supra, R. v. Miller, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 613,
49 C.R. (3d) 1, 16 Admin. L.R. 184, 3 C.C.C. (3d) 97, 24 D.L.R. (4th) 9, 14
O.A.C. 33, 63 N.R. 321, Cardinal v. Kent Inst., supra, and Morin v. Can. (Nat.
Special Handling Unit Review Ctee.), [1985] 2 S.C.R. 662, 49 C.R. (3d) 26, 16
Admin. L.R. 264, 23 C.C.C. (3d) 132, 24 D.L.R. (4th) 71, 63 N.R. 363 [Que.],
it can no longer be doubted that the decision to transfer an inmate to a penal
institution where his freedom will be more severely restricted is, in effect,
a committal to a "prison within a prison" which deprives the inmate of his liberty.
Such a decision must therefore, according to s. 7 of the Charter, be made "in
accordance with the principles of fundamental justice."

Section 7 of the Charter reads as follows:

Everyone has the right to life,liberty and security of the
person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the
principles of fundamental justice.

In Camphaug v. Canada (1990) 34 F.T.R. 165 (Fed. Ct.) Mr. Justice Strayer
referred to the requirement for fairness in decisions transferring prisoners
to higher security institutions. He said that:

This arises both under common law requirements of fairness
and under s. 7 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms which requires
that such decisions be taken in accordance with the principles of fundamental
justice....The content of the fairness requirement will be the same whether
it is ascribed to the common law or to the Charter. ... a decision to transfer
is not like a conviction for an offence: what is required on the part of the
decision-maker is a reasonable belief that the prisoner should be moved for
the sake of the institution.

Consequently, the decision to transfer Mr. Fitzgerald must meet the tests
of being fair pursuant to the principles enunciated under the Charter and of
not being patently unreasonable in a judicial review.

JUDGMENT

Mr. Conroy submitted that the decision to transfer was arbitrary, unreasonable,
a breach of fundamental justice and thereby unsupportable. In considering these
submissions I must keep in mind that the decision to transfer was made by authorities
who know infinitely more about the incarceration and corrections systems than
does the Court. The authorities should be secure to think that if they act in
accordance with fundamental principles of justice within the legislative requirements,
that their decisions will prevail.

In Plamondon v. W.C.B. (B.C.) et al. (1988) 21 B.C.L.R. (2d) 261 (S.C.B.C.),
Mr. Justice Shaw noted that there is a "delicate balance to be maintained" in
court reviews of the decisions of tribunals. He said that the courts must leave
a broad area within which statutory tribunals may operate and that this is why
"mere errors of law and fact are not reviewable. He continued:

It is desirable that the decisions of statutory tribunals,
wherever possible, be prompt and final. These tribunals often exercise jurisdiction
which involves specialized knowledge and skills that the courts do not have.

The courts should stand poised to intervene only in those cases wherein either
breaches of fundamental justice have occurred or where there is such unreasonableness
to a decision that it amounts to unfairness. The error should be egregious or
equivalent to bad faith: Syndicat des Employee de Production du Quebec v. C.L.R.B.
[1984] 2 S.C.R. 412. Even if the authorities are acting in an administrative
capacity, rather than a quasi-judicial one, they must act honestly and fairly.
This was commented upon by Roskill L.J. in Re Liverpool Taxi Owners' Association
[1972] 2 All E.R. 589 at 596: The power of the court to intervene is not limited,
as once was thought, to those cases where the function in question is judicial
or quasi-judicial. The modern cases show that this court will intervene more
widely than in the past. Even where the function is said to be administrative,
the court will not hesitate to intervene in a suitable case if it is necessary
in order to secure fairness.

In Martineau v. Matsqui Institution Disciplinary Board [1980] 1 S.C.R. 602
Dickson J. said at page 619:

When concerned with individual cases and aggrieved persons,
there is the tendency to forget that one is dealing with public law remedies,
which, when granted by the courts, not only set aright individual injustice,
but also ensure that public bodies exercising powers affecting citizens heed
the jurisdiction granted them. ... To give a narrow or technical interpretation
to "rights" in an individual sense is to misconceive the broader purpose of
judicial review of administrative action. One should, I suggest, begin with
the premise that any public body exercising power over subjects may be amenable
to judicial supervision, the individual interest involved being but one factor
to be considered in resolving the broad policy question of the nature of review
appropriate for the particular administrative body.

Within the confines of the "delicate balancing act" I have concerns about
the decision to transfer. One is the conclusion in the Assistant Deputy Commissioner's
reasons that he was "concerned about the level of risk Mr. Fitzgerald would
present to the community should he escape." The Case Management Team supported
Mr. Fitzgerald's transfer to Ferndale Institution, a minimum security facility.
It said as follows:

Mr. Fitzgerald has successfully cascaded through the judicial
system and he has taken appropriate programmes to correct and modify aberrant
behaviour patterns that lead to his criminal actions, and he has matured with
the passage of time. He feels that the time he spent attending programmes at
the Regional Psychiatric Centre led him to a clearer picture of his inadequacies
and the steps necessary to overcome them.

This makes it difficult to understand the basis for the conclusion of the
Assistant Deputy Commissioner which suggests some significant level of risk
to the public from Mr. Fitzgerald. There is nothing in the Reasons of Acting
Warden D.J. Denis about this. He simply upheld the transfer on the basis that
the "escape plot was very real."

Another of my concerns is the finding that the "escape plot was very real."
The plan is so inept as to put its existence into doubt. The petitioner was
to be picked up in a small boat off the shore of William Head Institution. The
likelihood of this event being observed seems significant. Mr. Fitzgerald was
then to make his way to Nanaimo and then, by way of B.C. Ferries, to Vancouver.
He would then, accompanied by his wife, depart on an international flight with
the ultimate destination being Costa Rica.

Mr. Denis found that the "explanation in relation to correspondence with various
countries as to residency is simply unbelievable, at best naive." There is,
in my opinion, a vast difference in the context of this case between "unbelievable"
and "naive". If it is unbelievable then Mr. Denis would be justified in concluding
that it was part of an escape plot. However, if it was simply naive then it
can have no bearing on the authenticity of the alleged plot.

The Assistant Deputy Commissioner said that the information from the informant,
an inmate, "is considered credible, primarily due to the level of detail provided.
Mr. Fitzgerald's escape attempt in 1984, and the discovered correspondence to
foreign consulates does support the belief that Mr. Fitzgerald was planning
to escape." The Assistant Deputy Commissioner then concluded that he is "not
convinced that the escape plot was a fabrication by the informant. Information
that would indicate the informant's motivation to provide false information
has not been presented."

It would be more convincing if he had concluded that the plot was a fabrication.
Furthermore, in that the authorities would not provide to the petitioner the
name of the informant, it was hardly open to the petitioner to provide "information
that would indicate the informant's motivation to provide false information."

There is also the problem created by the fact that much "material" is contained
in a sealed envelope. I am not suggesting that the contents should be revealed
to the petitioner. In any event, Mr. Justice Gow held that information has been
shared with the petitioner to the greatest extent possible. However, that does
not change the situation created by the concealment of information. There can
be no doubt but that the petitioner is in a disabled position to respond fully
to the concerns of the authorities.

This, in my opinion, puts more of an onus on the authorities to ensure that
the procedures followed are in keeping with the regulations and the procedures
set forth. Further, that the reasons given for decisions are sound and as revealing
as possible as to the foundations therefore. In this case I am unable to conclude
that the reasons properly reflect the evidence, including what is contained
in the sealed envelope.

While the authorities chose to find the informant reliable, and this is a
subjective judgment, I cannot uncover any basis for this. Nothing in the informant's
background suggests to me that he is a person to be relied upon.

The climate today is against a "soft" attitude towards prisoner's rights.
This is understandable in view of the crimes apparently committed by persons
on parole or who have escaped from correction institutions. However, I cannot
allow this atmosphere to govern the outcome of this petition.

It appears to me, in keeping with the submissions of counsel for the petitioner,
that the decisions to transfer have been extremely arbitrary and, as such, unfair
to the petitioner. The reasons and decisions do not conform to the principle
of natural justice which, after all, is only "fair play in action": Ridge v.
Baldwin [1963] 1 Q.B. 539, [1962] 1 All E.R. 834.

The decision to transfer is not, to again return to the words of Mr. Justice
Seaton, "a decision that we can let stand." I am of the opinion that it has
not been demonstrated that the decision was arrived at fairly. It is therefore
"patently unreasonable".

REMEDY

Counsel for the petitioner said that this court has broad discretion as to
the form of order. Counsel for the respondents submitted that it is only open
to order that the petitioner be returned to William Head Institution or that
the application be dismissed. He conceded that if there is a breach of fundamental
justice or if the decision is patently unreasonable, the petitioner deserves
a remedy.

Relying upon Gough v. Canada (National Parole Board), [1991] C.F. 171 (Fed.
C.A.), counsel for the petitioner submitted that Mr. Justice Gow erred in not
"finding a remedy for Mr. Fitzgerald." In Gough, Mahoney J.A., with whom the
other judges concurred, said at page 175:

I sympathize with the learned trial judge not wishing to
take the responsibility for putting a parolee back on the street when she knows
that there may be a good, but undivulged, reason by that would be unwise. As
she said, "To quash an order of the Parole Board, in a situation such as the
present, is not something which should be lightly undertaken." That, however,
misconceives the Court's responsibility. It is the responsibility of the Parole
Board both to take the parolee off the street and to justify that decision in
the context of the parolee's constitutional rights. If it fails to justify the
decision, when challenged, it is the Court's responsibility to give effect to
the parolee's constitutional rights.

In my respectful opinion, the Board is correct. An order
requiring the party that has been found to have infringed the constitutional
rights of another to produce evidence that may establish that the infringement
was justified, cannot, by any reasonable stretch of language or imagination,
be held to be a remedy, appropriate and just in the circumstances, granted to
the aggrieved party. [my underlining].

It is not for me to say what Mr. Justice Gow should have done. However, I
agree with the submissions as supported by the passages quoted from Gough. To
send the matter back to the respondents for yet another decision would not afford
Mr. Fitzgerald any remedy for the breaches that I have found to prevail. As
with the trial judge in Gough, to quash an order of the Assistant Deputy Commissioner
"is not something that should be lightly undertaken." However, that decision
has been challenged and, in my opinion, successfully.

The only appropriate remedy available is to return Mr. Fitzgerald to William
Head Institution.
